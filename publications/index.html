Research

Social Protection

  1. Government Scheme Overload: Take-Up and Targeting in a Multi-Scheme Context
    Mitali Roy Mathur
    Presented at: Advances in Field Experiments (2026), GPRL-IPA Research Gathering (2026), CEGA Staff Meeting (2025)
    Funding: CEGA ($25,800), UC Davis Economics Department ($13,600), J-PAL Social Protection Initiative ($5,175)
    Research question: How does the quantity and quality of government welfare schemes offered to individuals affect take-up and welfare?
    Methods: Randomized Controlled Trial in India
    Keywords: Social Protection Policies, Take-Up, Targeting, Choice Overload
    Abstract Press

    Governments in developing countries often offer a variety of social protection programs to their citizens, but take-up rates remain quite low. While researchers have studied interventions meant to improve the take-up and targeting of specific welfare schemes, little is known about how individuals navigate the entire system of welfare schemes and decide which ones to apply to. Exposure to a large quantity of welfare benefits can have countervailing effects. On the one hand, when individuals have many welfare schemes to choose from, they can make a targeted choice in applying to the benefit(s) that fit their needs the most. On the other hand, they might be overwhelmed by the sheer quantity of schemes they could apply to and not apply to any, leading them to miss out on key benefits. Therefore, studying how individuals make decisions on how many and which welfare schemes to apply to can shed light on the role that behavioral elements of choice play in a social protection setting. I will implement a randomized controlled trial that varies the number of benefits frontline workers share with informal workers in India, a country with thousands of welfare schemes. I conducted a similar pilot study with over 2,000 workers in New Delhi and found that on average, individuals went on to apply to 1 scheme upon learning about their eligibility. People who learned about one-scheme applied to 0.203 more schemes than did people who learned about multiple-schemes, translating to 498 more schemes availed. I will refine and scale this study to gather more evidence on "scheme overload" and investigate its welfare effects by analyzing how the quantity of schemes shared impacts the quantity and match-quality of schemes availed. My research highlights the importance of studying take-up in multi-scheme settings and motivates policies related to scheme convergence.

Migration

  1. Ahead with H-4 EAD: The Effects of Granting Work Authorization to High-Skilled Female Immigrants
    Mitali Roy Mathur
    Presented at: Economics of Migration Junior Workshop (2026), Junior Economics of Migration Zoom Seminar (2026)
    Research question: What are the labor market, household, and selection effects of offering work authorization to the spouses of H-1B workers?
    Methods: Natural Language Processing, K-Means Clustering, Difference-in-Differences
    Keywords: High-skilled immigration, Joint migration, H-1B visa, H-4 visa
    Abstract

    Countries often host immigrants under a variety of visa categories. Immigration policy strictly regulates the quantity, eligibility requirements, and rights of immigrants in each visa group. Given norms around family unification, many immigrant visa categories include visas for dependents. Policymakers and researchers typically focus on studying the effects of immigration driven by lead migrants. Com- paratively less attention has focused on the dependents of these migrants. Understanding how policy changes impact immigrants on dependent visas plays an important role in studying the joint decisions migrants make. In this paper, I evaluate the effects of a 2015 change in immigration policy in the U.S. that allowed H-4 visa holders (spouses of high-skilled H-1B holders) access to employment authorization (EAD). I use natural language processing (NLP) and unsupervised machine learning (ML) to identify likely H-1B and H-4 visa holders in the American Community Survey. After validating my classification approach through a series of checks, I use a difference-in-differences strategy to isolate the policy’s labor market and family effects. I find that the work authorization restriction created a latent labor supply of high-skilled female immigrants that were ready to work, but were legally barred from doing so. One year after the policy removed these restrictions, the employment rate of H-4 visa holders increased by 42.2 percentage points relative to similar naturalized citizens, effectively closing the employment gap between citizens and H-4 visa holders. I also find that the H-4 EAD policy increased the fertility rate of H-4 visa holders by 6.3 percentage points relative to similar non-citizens. My results are driven by incumbent H-4 visa holders who were already in the U.S. prior to the policy change, indicating that the mechanism was not selection. Additional descriptive evidence suggests that the H-4 EAD policy did not change the composition of H-1B or H-4 workers. Overall, my research uses the example of the change in policy impacting H-4 visa holders to show how a simple machine-learning-based approach to visa classification can be used to analyze the effects of specific immigration policies. I find that small changes to the legal rights of dependents have short-term effects in realigning labor supply and family formation towards equilibrium, without altering the composition of new immigrants.

  2. The Immigration Impact on Population, Labor Productivity, Investments and TFP in OECD Countries
    Gaetano Basso, Mitali Roy Mathur, and Giovanni Peri
    Research question: What drives immigration across countries, and what are the macroeconomic effects of immigration in OECD countries?
    Methods: Local projections, Shift-share IV
    Keywords: OECD Economies, Immigration, Population Change, Immigration Policies, Productivity, Investment, Employment-Population Ratio
    Abstract Paper

    OECD countries, and particularly Europe’s leading economies, have experienced a significant decline in native population growth alongside large increases in net immigration. We compile a new and comprehensive dataset of net immigration rates from all origins to OECD countries and document the characteristics of these flows, establishing that most of the net immigration increase was from non-OECD countries and was significantly high-skilled, over the period 1990–2024. We show that the substantial cross-country heterogeneity in immigration dynamics is difficult to explain by push factors or network effects, while immigration policy – as captured by summary indices – account for only a small share of the observed variation. Unexpected immigration shocks and surges were relevant phenomena of these years. We then examine the relationship between immigration and growth in population and GDP per capita, focusing on labor productivity, capital investment, and total factor productivity (TFP), using local projections and several sources of identifying variation. We find that immigration from non-OECD countries has been a significant driver of GDP per worker growth, primarily through higher investment. High-skilled immigration, in particular, is associated with stronger human capital accumulation, faster TFP growth, and greater capital deepening. Positive immigration shocks and surges and more open immigration policies, are likewise associated with improved economic performance. By contrast, native population growth has either no effect or a weakly negative effect on GDP per capita and productivity growth. The estimated coefficients on immigration, while positive, are often imprecisely estimated. Still, their magnitude and direction are important and consistent with a large and growing literature documenting the positive effects of immigration – especially high-skilled immigration – on productivity, entrepreneurship, and economic growth.

  3. Landscaping internal migrant vulnerabilities and labor department schemes
    Mitali Roy Mathur
    Research question: Which government schemes are most important to internal migrants in India?
    Methods: Qualitative interviews
    Keywords: Internal Migration, Social Protection Programs, Vulnerability
    Abstract

    I led a research project in collaboration with the research team at Jan Sahas’s Migrants Resilience Collaborative. In this project, I built a database of different labor department welfare schemes in India. Next, I constructed a unique vulnerability index using a weighting technique to rank schemes based on vulnerabilities migrants experience tied to their demographic profiles. Finally, I designed an application in which users can enter different demographic profiles of migrants and see a ranking of types of schemes that may be most important to that migrant persona. This research contributes to the literature on optimal program design with respect to welfare targeting and lays the foundation for future research into interventions that can increase internal migrants’ take-up of specific schemes.

Gig Work

  1. Uncertainty and Frictions in Gig Work: Evidence via Conditional Income Guarantees
    Mitali Roy Mathur, Aleksandr Michuda, and Shotaro Nakamura
    Funding: IDinsight Digital Economy Research Impact Initiative ($200,000)
    Research question: How do conditional guarantees in the gig economy affect labor supply, earnings, and welfare?
    Methods: BDM auction, MTE, field experiment
    Keywords: Willingness to pay, contract design, gig work
    Abstract Read More

    Gig economy workers face a fundamental trade-off between the flexibility of piece-rate payment and the uncertainty of earnings, yet causal evidence on how workers value this trade-off remains scarce. We propose a field experiment with ride-hailing drivers in which each driver states their willingness-to-pay (WTP) for four fixed guarantee contracts varying in trip requirements, scheduling flexibility, and guarantee personalization. A random price P ∗ is drawn from a continuous distribution; drivers whose WTP meets or exceeds P ∗ are enrolled at that price, generating a continuously distributed instrument that identifies marginal treatment effects (MTEs) for the full sample. Post-enrollment, a Karlan-Zinman surprise randomization assigns enrolled drivers to improved or unchanged contract terms, providing clean identification of moral hazard separate from selection. The design enables us to: (i) measure money-metric valuations of labor supply costs, scheduling flexibility, and guarantee personalization through within-driver bid differences; (ii) separate adverse selection from moral hazard using WTP variation among enrolled drivers and the post-enrollment surprise randomization; (iii) estimate a full MTE curve over the entire population of drivers; and (iv) conduct structural welfare analysis including optimal contract design, mandatory versus voluntary enrollment comparisons, and personalized guarantee simulations

  2. Understanding the challenges of female surveyors
    Mitali Roy Mathur, and Lipika Biswas
    Research question: What are the unique challenges that female surveyors face?
    Methods: Descriptive survey
    Keywords: Female surveyors, fieldwork, gig work
    Abstract Read More

    I co-led an internal project with a field manager at IDinsight to understand the challenges that female surveyors face.     Methods: Web-scraping, Difference=in-differences
    Keywords: Female labor force participation, Academic conferences
    Abstract Data

  2. Evaluating the implementation of a women’s empowerment program
    IDinsight
    Research question: Did IWWAGE’s women’s empowerment program achieve its intended outcomes?
    Methods: Descriptive survey, process evaluation
    Keywords: Process evaluation, women’s empowerment
    Abstract

    I led the first of two rounds of a process evaluation to understand the implementation of the Strengthening Women’s Institutions for Agency and Empowerment program run by the Institute for What Works to Advance Gender Equality (IWWAGE). The program is implemented by different partners in four states and focuses on establishing gender resource centers and introducing training programs for women in self help groups. The first round entailed detailing out a theory of change to understand the program and collecting data via a phone survey on key process indicators. The results from the second round highlight the opportunities for IWWAGE to improve its program implementation while scaling.

    Research question: How can we improve response rates in phone surveys in developing contexts?
    Methods: Phone survey
    Keywords: Phone Surveys, Response rates, Data quality
    Abstract Read More Paper

    During the COVID-19 pandemic, in-person primary data collection was put on hold.   2. Evaluating the effectiveness of behavioral nudges at the honest reporting of COVID-19 symptoms
    IDinsight
    Research question: Can behavior nudges improve the honest reporting of COVID-19 symptoms?
    Methods: Randomized controlled trial
    Keywords: COVID-19, behavioral nudges, self-reporting
    Abstract Read More

    I worked on the analysis of a randomized controlled trial that IDinsight and Praekelt designed to understand the role of behavioral nudges in the honest reporting of COVID-19 symptoms by university-goers in South Africa. We found that an appeal for university-goers to be honest was most effective at increasing honesty in mobile HealthCheck daily self-assessment surveys. The results of this RCT highlight the small impacts that low-cost, digital interventions can have.
